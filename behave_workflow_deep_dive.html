STEP 0 — BEFORE ANYTHING ELSE
0

Reading the Ad

● claude-sonnet-4-20250514

Before any persona sees the ad, Claude reads it like a human would. You only provide the brand name and the image. Everything else is extracted automatically.

Analogy: Think of this as a smart assistant who watches the ad and fills in the briefing sheet for you — so you don't have to type "Headline: Khushiyon ki Safedii" manually into 20 fields.

What it extracts from the image

● Category (e.g. FMCG)
● Headline & body text
● Price shown / discount %
● Trust signals
● Urgency cues
● Visual style
● Is a celebrity present?
● Hindi / Hinglish / English?
● Festival context (Diwali?)
● Brand era (heritage vs challenger)
● Marketing tone (emotional vs functional)
● Brand positioning (premium vs value)

Output: a CreativeCard

A structured data object with ~25 fields. Every downstream step reads from this — personas never see the raw image directly (unless vision is enabled).

CreativeCard { brand: "Nirma" category: "fmcg_household" headline: "Khushiyon ki Safedii" price_shown: null brand_era: "heritage" ← NEW field marketing_tone: "emotional" ← NEW field trust_signals: ["no chemicals"] emotional_hooks: ["family", "nostalgia"] }

Why brand_era matters

If Claude identifies the brand as heritage, the persona evaluator later knows to surface childhood memories about that brand. A persona who grew up with Nirma reacts totally differently to a Nirma ad than to a challenger brand ad from 2023.

Skipped when?

If you type the creative details manually in the form, Step 0 is skipped entirely. The image extractor is a convenience — not a requirement.

STEP 1 — WHO WATCHES THE AD?
1

Cohort Selection

● No AI · Pure math

The system picks which personas evaluate this ad. You choose how many (1–20) and how they're selected.

The 3 selection modes

AUTO Best Match (default)

The system scores every persona against the category and picks the highest-scorers. This is the "best 5" mode — not random, not handpicked. Best for getting realistic results.

MANUAL You Pick Personas

You select checkboxes from the persona list. Use this when you know exactly who your target audience is — e.g. "only Tier-2 city homemakers."

Analogy: A focus group facility calls people from a database. "Best match" means the recruiter picks people who've bought in this category before. "Manual" means the client says "I want these specific demographics."

How "Best Match" scoring works

Every persona has a list of category affinities — how interested they are in FMCG, Electronics, Fashion, etc. on a 0–1 scale. The scoring formula is simple:

persona_score = (category_interest × 5.0) ← main signal + archetype_diversity_bonus ← ensures variety

Then all personas are sorted by this score and the top N are picked.

Archetype diversity — why it matters

If all 5 personas were "brand loyalist", your results would be biased. The system deliberately ensures that different types of people are represented:

💸 Impulse Buyer
🔍 Pragmatist
❤️ Brand Loyalist
✨ Aspirational Buyer
⚖️ Price Anchor
🤔 Value Seeker
Bottom line: "Best 5" means the 5 people with the highest category interest who together represent the widest spread of buying personalities. Not random. Not manual. Optimally representative.
STEP 2 — WHAT EACH PERSON CARRIES IN THEIR HEAD
2

Memory Loading

● No AI · Load from disk

Each selected persona's memory file is loaded. These files grow over time — every simulation run adds to them.

The 5 memory types — what gets stored

SCENE
Formative era memory — loaded at persona birth, never changes. "Sunday 1988, Nani Ji, the Nirma jingle on Doordarshan." High emotional charge. This is new in this version.
OBSERVATION
Every ad evaluation ever done. "I saw a Surf Excel FMCG ad 3 days ago. I would scroll past. The price felt too premium."
PREFERENCE
Created only when score > 70. "I genuinely liked the Myntra fashion ad. The pricing was transparent and felt fair to me."
BELIEF
Created only when score < 30. "I was put off by the Lenskart ad. Something about the hard sell reminded me of MLM tactics."
REFLECTION
Higher-order insight. "I tend to distrust skincare brands that rely on celebrity endorsements without clinical backing." Synthesised from multiple observations.

NEW: Scene Memories (Manufacturing Nostalgia)

Before this version, nostalgia was stored as a trait list: iconic_ads: ["Nirma jingle", "Air India Maharaja"]. That's just a label. The LLM treated it like a tag, not a memory.

Now, each persona gets one or more scene memories — full first-person narratives:

"It was Sunday afternoon. Nani Ji had just finished braiding my hair with jasmine oil. The black-and-white Doordarshan set was on. Then the Nirma jingle started — washing powder Nirma... She hummed along without even noticing. That sound and that smell are fused in my head forever."

This is what makes the persona feel the nostalgia rather than just mention it.

The 3 nostalgia layers

1.
Sensory anchor — the specific trigger: jasmine oil smell, jingle melody, the white powder cloud. This is what an ad today might accidentally reactivate.
2.
Life context — who was there, what they felt, what they didn't know yet. Emotional weight comes from who was present.
3.
Present contrast — the gap between then and now. Nani Ji is gone. The jingle is still there. That gap is the emotional charge.
STEP 3 — THE SMART MEMORY PICKER
3

Memory Retrieval

● No AI · Weighted scoring

With 100 memories, how does the system decide which 5 to put in the prompt? It scores every single memory against 5 dimensions and picks the highest scorers.

The scoring formula

score = α × recency + β × relevance + γ × importance + δ × emotional_arousal ← NEW + ε × log(1 + access_count) ← NEW

All 5 scores combined → one number per memory → sort → take top 5.

α · Recency
Memories from yesterday beat memories from 6 months ago — unless their other scores are very high. Exponential decay over hours.
w=1.0
β · Relevance
Text similarity between the memory and the current ad being evaluated. A Nirma memory scores high for a Surf Excel ad. A Zomato memory scores low.
w=1.0
γ · Importance
How significant was this experience when it happened? Scored 0–10. A strong emotional reaction scores 8. A neutral scroll-past scores 3.
w=1.0
δ · Emotional Arousal NEW
The emotional charge of the memory. Scene memories start at 0.85–0.95. Recent neutral ad evaluations start at 0.1–0.3. This is why a 1988 memory beats a Tuesday memory.
w=1.5
ε · Retrieval Frequency NEW
Memories that get retrieved often become more dominant. Uses log(1 + count) so the boost has diminishing returns — the 100th recall doesn't add as much as the 2nd.
w=0.3

Scope — the pre-filter before scoring

Before scoring, the system first filters which memories are even eligible. This is the "scope" option in the dashboard:

NONE Ignore all memory. Persona sees the ad with zero history — like a first-time customer. Use for baseline testing.
CATEGORY Default. Only memories from the same industry — FMCG memories for an FMCG ad, Electronics for Electronics. Cross-category reflections are also included.
BRAND Only memories about this specific brand. "What does this persona think specifically about Nirma?" Best for brand health studies.
FULL Everything the persona has ever experienced. Slow and expensive to process — only use when you're testing for cross-category spillover effects.

The auto-strategy switch

After scope filtering, if a persona has fewer than 50 category memories, the system just dumps all of them into the prompt. Why? Because when there are only 10–15 memories, the scoring formula adds noise — better to just show everything.

Once they cross 50 memories? The scoring formula kicks in and picks the best 5. This mirrors how streaming systems work — backfill everything early, then switch to live scoring at scale.

Memory gets stronger on each retrieval

Every time a memory is picked and shown to the LLM, it gets a tiny boost: arousal += 0.02, importance += 0.1. The more a childhood Nirma memory surfaces, the more dominant it becomes over time — exactly how nostalgia works in real humans.

STEP 4 — THE PERSON REACTS TO THE AD
4

Persona Evaluation

● claude-sonnet-4-20250514

A carefully constructed prompt goes to Claude. It tells Claude who to be, what they remember, and then shows them the ad. Claude responds in-character.

Anatomy of the prompt (in order)

SYSTEM INSTRUCTION (NEW)
"If this creative triggers a personal memory from your past — let that memory surface naturally BEFORE you analyze. Real consumers do not separate emotion from evaluation."
WHO YOU ARE
Name, age, city, income, occupation, family status
YOUR FORMATIVE MEMORIES (NEW — appears FIRST)
First-person scene narratives. Were here before, now moved to the top.
YOUR PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY
Price sensitivity 0.7 · Brand loyalty 0.4 · Impulse tendency 0.6...
YOUR PAST EXPERIENCES
Top 5 retrieved memories — recent ad reactions, preferences, beliefs
THE AD YOU ARE SEEING
The CreativeCard data — brand, headline, price, visuals, tone...

What Claude returns

{ "primary_action": "engage", "sentiment": "positive", "overall_score": 72, "attention_score": 7.5, "relevance_score": 6.8, "trust_score": 5.2, "desire_score": 6.0, "clarity_score": 8.5, "first_impression": "Yaar, Nirma ka naam sunte hi...", "reasoning": "The jingle in the video reminded me...", "objections": ["powder quality has declined"], "verbatim_reaction": "Arre, ye dekh ke toh Nani Ji yaad aa gayi. Sunday mornings aur woh jingle — aaj bhi dil mein hai.", "importance_score": 8.5, "key_themes": ["nostalgia", "family", "trust"] }
Why "verbatim_reaction" matters: This is the WhatsApp voice note the persona would send their friend. Not "I found the advertisement emotionally resonant." But "Arre yaar, ye wahi Nirma hai na jo Nani ke zamaane mein hoti thi?" The difference is everything.
The ordering matters. Scene memories appear at the top of the prompt — before numbers, before scores, before psychology. LLMs pay more attention to content that appears early. Before this change, a scene memory buried under 80 lines of data was effectively invisible. Now it leads.
STEP 5 + 6 — THE QUALITY GATE AND THE MEMORY COMMIT
5

The Critic Gate

● claude-sonnet-4-20250514

A second Claude call immediately reads the evaluation and asks one question: Is this realistic? This is the quality police. It's completely separate from the evaluator.

4 checks the critic runs

1.
Persona consistency — Does a price-sensitive Indore homemaker on ₹30k/month really give an "I'll buy this immediately" to a ₹5000 product? Red flag if yes.
2.
Sycophancy detection — LLMs tend to be unrealistically positive. If every persona scored 80+, something is wrong. The critic catches "generic enthusiasm."
3.
Cultural authenticity — Does the verbatim actually sound like someone from that city/background? "I found this creatively stimulating" is not how someone from Patna talks.
4.
Action-reasoning alignment — "I'm worried about the price and I don't trust this brand" → primary_action: "purchase_intent" is a contradiction. Flag it.
If PASS
Evaluation is counted in the scorecard ✓
If FAIL
Quarantined — excluded from results. Still saved for audit. ✗
6

Memory Commit

● No AI · Save to file

Only critic-approved evaluations become memories. Garbage in, garbage out — the critic gate protects memory quality.

What gets written to memory

OBSERVATION Always written. "I saw a Nirma FMCG ad. My first impression: reminded me of childhood. I would engage."
PREFERENCE Only if overall_score > 70. Captures lasting brand affinity.
BELIEF Only if overall_score < 30. Captures lasting brand distrust or category skepticism.

Step 7 (conditional): Reflection

● claude-haiku-3-20250307

When a persona's total accumulated importance from memories crosses 50 points, a reflection is triggered. The 10 most important memories are gathered and Haiku is asked:

"Given these experiences, what high-level insight can you draw about your own buying patterns?"

The result is a REFLECTION node: "I distrust skincare brands that lead with before/after photos over ingredient transparency." This then gets retrieved in future evals and influences future reactions.

Analogy: Reflection is like when you notice, after 5 bad dates, "I keep falling for people who are emotionally unavailable." You didn't know it after date 1. After date 5, the pattern is obvious. That insight now changes how date 6 goes.

The 5 possible decisions

ITERATE Good bones, needs specific changes before scaling.

Analogy: This is the media planner sitting at the table after the focus group. She's not just reading results. She's saying "Run Creative B. Kill A. For Creative B, change the CTA — that's what's holding back conversion intent."
THE BIG CHANGE — MANUFACTURING NOSTALGIA

What changed — and why it matters

Nostalgia was already in the data. It was just not making it into the output.

The core problem: The old system stored cultural references as a list — iconic_ads: ["Nirma jingle"]. That's a label. A Post-It note. The AI read it and produced analyst language: "This ad appeals to nostalgia which is effective for this demographic." Technically true. Emotionally hollow.

WHAT BEFORE — v1 AFTER — v2 (Manufacturing Nostalgia)
How nostalgia was stored Trait list: iconic_ads: ["Nirma jingle"] — a tag, not a story First-person narrative scene with sensory detail, who was there, emotional charge — a lived experience
Where it appeared in the prompt Buried at the bottom under ## Your Cultural Memory — after 80 lines of psychology numbers. LLMs pay less attention to content that appears late. Now the first thing after identity — before price sensitivity, before scores. Leads the context. Gets attended to.
Memory retrieval priority A memory from 1988 competed equally with a neutral ad memory from Tuesday. Same weights = old memories lose to recent ones. emotional_arousal=0.85 × weight 1.5x. A high-arousal childhood memory beats a flat recent one on the scoring formula — even if it's 40 years older.
System instruction "If a brand triggers a childhood memory — say so." "If this creative triggers a personal memory — let it surface BEFORE you analyze. Real consumers don't separate emotion from evaluation."
What the LLM actually produced "This ad appeals to nostalgia which is effective for this demographic. The brand heritage creates trust signals." "Yaar, ye Nirma wali dhun sunte hi Nani Ji yaad aa gayi — Sunday mornings, oiled hair, Doordarshan. Dil bhar aaya. Definitely buying."
Memory gets stronger over time? No. Every retrieval was stateless — no impact on future retrievals. Yes. Each retrieval boosts arousal += 0.02, importance += 0.1. The more it surfaces, the more dominant it becomes.

Why this is the right fix: The problem was architectural, not content-based. The personas were rich. The memories were detailed. But the prompt structure had nostalgia losing to recency in the retrieval formula, and arriving too late in the prompt to fully shape the LLM's character. Both are now fixed.
